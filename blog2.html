Why Cross Border Contracts Fail and How to Protect Your Revenue

Introduction

Cross border contracts are the backbone of international trade, yet a surprising number of them fail to protect the parties involved. When businesses operate across different legal systems, poorly drafted agreements can lead to revenue loss, delivery disputes, and unenforceable obligations. This article identifies the most common failure points in international contracts and explains how to draft agreements that protect your revenue.

Unclear Governing Law

One of the most frequent mistakes in cross border contracts is failing to specify which country's laws will govern the agreement. Without a clear governing law clause, parties may face conflicting interpretations from courts in different jurisdictions. A well drafted contract explicitly states the governing law and ensures that the chosen jurisdiction has a developed body of commercial law relevant to the transaction.

Weak Payment Terms

Ambiguous payment terms create significant risk in international transactions. Contracts must clearly define the currency of payment, payment milestones, late payment penalties, and the mechanism for currency conversion. When payment terms are vague, international buyers may delay payments without consequence, severely impacting the seller's cash flow.

Poor Delivery Terms

Delivery obligations in international contracts must account for shipping timelines, customs clearance, insurance coverage, and inspection rights. Vague language such as "reasonable delivery time" creates disputes. Instead, contracts should specify exact delivery windows, the party responsible for shipping costs, and the point at which risk transfers from seller to buyer.

Missing Incoterms Alignment

The International Commercial Terms published by the ICC provide standardized definitions for common trade terms such as FOB, CIF, and DAP. Contracts that fail to reference specific Incoterms leave room for conflicting interpretations about cost allocation and risk transfer. Properly incorporating Incoterms 2020 into your agreements significantly reduces delivery disputes.

💡 Key Insight: Always specify the Incoterms version year in your contract. Terms like "FOB Incoterms 2020" eliminate ambiguity about which set of rules applies.

No Dispute Resolution Clause

International litigation is expensive and unpredictable. Contracts that lack a clear dispute resolution mechanism force parties into costly court battles. A strong arbitration clause specifying the institution such as the ICC, LCIA, or DIAC and the seat of arbitration provides a more predictable and enforceable path for resolving disagreements.

Weak Intellectual Property Protection

For technology companies and digital exporters, intellectual property provisions are among the most critical sections of any contract. Weak IP clauses can result in the loss of proprietary rights across jurisdictions. Contracts must clearly define ownership of pre existing IP, newly developed IP, and the scope of any licenses granted to the counterparty.

Final Checklist

  • Include an explicit governing law and jurisdiction clause.
  • Define payment currency, milestones, and late payment consequences.
  • Specify delivery terms with clear timelines and risk transfer points.
  • Incorporate the appropriate Incoterms 2020 designation.
  • Add a binding arbitration clause with a recognized institution.
  • Protect intellectual property with clear ownership and licensing terms.
  • Have the contract reviewed by a cross border legal consultant before signing.
